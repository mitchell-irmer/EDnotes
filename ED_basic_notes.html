LECTURE NOTES

  • Federal gov increasingly dependent on industry investments and commercial tech
  • Federally Funded Research and Development Centers (FFRDC)
    • Drive innovation and problem solving by assisting the Government in conducting research, technology development, systems acquisition and policy guidance
  • University Affiliated Research Centers
    • special type of FFRDC
    • SCAR, GIT, MIT, JHAPL, PSU
  • TRLs
    • 1. Basic principles observed and reported
    • 2. Technology concept and/ or application formulated
    • 3. Analytical and experimental critical function and/or characteristic proof of concept
    • 4. Component and/or breadboard validation in laboratory environment
    • 5. Component and/or breadboard validation in relevant environment
    • 6. System/subsystem model demonstration in relevant environment
    • 7.System prototype demonstration in relevant environment
    • 8. Actual system completed and qualified through test and demonstration
    • 9. Integration is mission proven through successful mission operations
  • Joint Capability Tech Demo (JCTD)
    • evaluate military utility
    • develop CONOPS and doctrine

SUMMARY

  • What is the U.S. technology posture in relation to defense acquisition?

    – Rely heavily on industry research and commercial technologies

  • What is the role of S&T in defense acquisitions?

    – Perform basic and applied research to address battlefield deficiencies and mature technologies to reduce risk for system integration

  • When during the acquisition life‐cycle are we most likely to introduce new technologies?

    – Prior to M/S B

  • Key characteristics of ATD?

    – Assess feasibility and maturity of technology, risk reducer

  • Key characteristics of JCTD?

    – Evaluate military utility, develop CONOPS & doctrine, Go to War capability

3.6.1 (TLO 3,5b,8,13)

TLOs

  • 1. Recognize the concept of Earned Value Management (EVM).
  • 2. Recognize how EVM fits into the defense acquisition process.
    • a. Recognize under which conditions EVM is required based on governing EVM statutes, regulatory policy, and DoD implementation guidance.
    • b. Recognize the value and benefits of EVM in the defense acquisition process.
  • 3. Recognize the importance of Earned Value as a management tool.
  • 4. Recognize the management processes associated with EVM.
  • 5. Recognize the key processes in the development and management of a Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB).
    • a. Identify the relationship of the PMB to program objectives.
    • b. Identify the sequence of steps in the development of the initial PMB.
  • 6. Identify the three major reasons for PMB changes, their impact, and the approval authority for each.
  • 7. Identify the benefits of reviewing the PMB at the Integrated Baseline Review (IBR) to evaluate its validity.
  • 8. Identify the DoD offices and agencies responsible for EVM.
  • 9. Recognize required EVM implementation activities.
  • 10. Recognize the purpose of EVM reviews (Integrated Baseline Review (IBR), Initial Compliance Evaluation, and Post Acceptance Review) and when they must be completed.
  • 11. Identify performance report tailoring considerations and their effect on reporting.
  • 12. Identify and understand the purpose, formats, and content of the Integrated Program Management Data Analysis Report (IPMDAR).
  • 13. Identify the primary factors that the government should review to evaluate the contractor’s PMB during an IBR.
  • 14. Recognize the importance of Earned Value data in external reporting.
  • 15. Recall the importance of the Contractor Funds Status Report (CFSR) and the differences between the CFSR and the IPMDAR.

LECTURE NOTES

  • Purpose of EVM
    • It is a management tool that helps monitor, control, and report the contract and/or project performance
    • Many benefits, including better overall planning and control discipline for proactive decision making and risk management decisions.
  • Earned Value equations always start with EV
  • Definitions
    • PV = BCWS (BUDGETED COST OF WORK SCHEDULED)
    • EV = BCWP (BCW PERFORMED)
    • AC = ACWP (ACTUAL CWP)
  • EVM must be used for:
    • contracts > $20M
    • must be formally validated by DCMA for > $100M
  • EVM discouraged for FFP contracts
  • EVM principal players
    • DCMA ensures EVMS surveillance and compliance with DoD directives and contractual requirements; DCAA assists with audits
    • At SUPSHIP, DCMA/DCAA roles are filled by the command
  • Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB)
    • the S-curve graph of money vs time
    • time-phased plan for accomplishing authorized work
  • PMB Development Steps
    • Before the contract award
      • develop prelim schedule
      • estimate resources required to do the work
      • may build in cushion for risk
  • Management Reserve
    • funds set aside for unknown but in-scope tasks
    • should be tied to risk areas
  • Integrated Baseline Review (IBR)
    • Joint assessment conducted by the Government PM and Contractor to verify realism and accuracy of the PMB;
    • ensuring it captures the entire technical scope, schedule requirements, and is adequately resourced
    • done with 6 months of contract award
    • management reserve assessed with respect to program risk
  • Integrated Program Management Data Analysis Report (IPMDAR)
    • 6 formats (specifics don't matter now, but could be Work Breakdown Structure (WBS))

SUMMARY

  • What is the PMB?

    – A time‐phased plan that represents all work on the contract

  • What are the steps in developing the PMB?

    – 1. Define scope of work to the control account level – 2. Schedule the work in logical sequence – 3. Budget for the work – 3a. Time‐phase the budget

  • What are the three reasons for PMB changes that we discussed?

    – Formal contract changes – Internal re‐planning – Formal reprogramming (OTB)

  • When is EVM required in DoD?

    – Cost or Incentive contracts & subcontracts greater than 20 million in then‐year dollars (Implement ANSI/EIA‐748) – Cost or Incentive contracts & subcontracts greater than 100 million in then‐year dollars (Implement ANSI/EIA‐748 & a formally validated EVM System) – Contracts below these thresholds – EVM is optional

3.6.2 (TLO 1,2,4,6)

TLOs

  • 1. Define the 5 Earned Value Management (EVM) independent variables (BCWS, BCWP, ACWP, BAC, EAC) and use them to compute appropriate performance status indicators.
  • 2. Identify the limitations in analyzing cost and schedule variance.
  • 3. Identify the four EVM data analysis steps.
  • 4. Recognize five EVM signs of significant problem areas or risk.
  • 5. Match the type of work with the most appropriate performance measurement technique.
  • 6. Given a Gantt chart and performance (e.g., cost, risk and schedule) data, detect and analyze the impact of significant problem areas, based on the status indicators.
  • 7 Analyze and interpret Contractor performance indicators (cost variance, schedule variance, SPI & CPI) to explain the program's cost and schedule status and identify trends and problem.
  • 8. Given performance data, calculate an estimate of cost at completion.
    • a. Understand difference between Government and Contractor estimates at completion
  • 9. Analyze the Contractor’s status by applying earned value analysis techniques (e.g., calculate cost variance, schedule variance, cost performance index and schedule performance index).
  • 10. Given a segment of contract work and associated tasks, plan and schedule the tasks and resources necessary to complete contract work within cost and schedule constraints.

LECTURE NOTES

  • If EVM is required, know to look for a CDRL.
  • Graph hints
    • When BCWP is under BCWS on graph, behind schedule
    • When ACWP is over BCWP on graph, over budget
  • Apply graph hints to equations to determine cost/performance (PDF page 1505/lecture slide 7)
  • Schedule variance:
    • by itself gives no info about critical path
    • needs other schedule info for analysis
    • may be misleading (favorable offsets unfavorable)
  • Cost variance:
    • further analysis necessary to determine causes of cost variance and overall effect on contract cost
    • trend indicator
    • used in top level reporting to assess if budget is reasonable
    • may be misleading when comparing $$ to man-hours
  • SPI and CPI are fractions, CV and PV are pos/neg numbers.
  • Variance at Completion (VAC)
    • VAC = BAC - EAC (Budget at Completion - Estimate at Completion)
  • Common indicators of problem areas or risks
    • Application of management reserves
    • Significant revisions of PMB
    • Zero variance
    • Sudden change in monthly performance trends
    • Linear downward cost variance
    • Unreasonable TCPI (To Complete Performance Index)

SUMMARY

  • What are the 4 Data Analysis Steps?

    – Get current performance status – Identify performance trends – Predict performance completion – Determine management actions

  • What are the 4 Discrete Effort Performance Measurement Techniques and when are they used?

    – Percent Complete Method (long duration tasks with no milestones) – Weighted Milestones (long duration tasks with identifiable milestones) – Percent Start/ Percent Complete (short duration tasks < 2 reporting periods) – 0/100 Method

  • (True/False) A major limitation of cost variance is that it does not take critical path into account

    – FALSE – that is a limitation of schedule variance

LECTURE NOTES

  • Total Ship Readiness Assessments
    • last chance to add work to work package
  • Integrated Schedule
    • most important part of the availability
  • RMC contracting strategy – FIRM FIXED PRICE

SUMMARY

  • What are the five sources of AWP work candidates?

    – Class Maintenance Plan (CMP) or Integrated CMP (ICMP) – Authorized alterations (Program and Fleet) – TYCOM repairs – Results of pre‐availability test and inspections – Current Ship Maintenance Project (CSMP)

  • Who produces the Baseline AWP for submarines, carriers, and surface ships?

    – Submarines: SUBMEPP – Carriers: PMS 312 (Carrier Planning Activity) – Surface: SURFMEPP

  • What is the most important product of the planning process?

    – Integrated Schedule

  • What is a component unit (CU)?

    – A physical piece of equipment or hardware that is the unit of work control

  • Who is the major Public Shipyard management team member during availability planning?

    – Project Engineering and Planning Manager (PEPM)

  • What are the planning goals to be met prior to starting an availability?

    – Ordering long lead‐time material – Special tooling on‐site – Early start requirements – Critical path/key events known and included in the integrated schedule – Logistics concerns addressed – Training for crew on shipyard procedures – On‐time contract award or definitization (RMC)

  • Why did surface ship maintenance contracting revert to FFP under the MACMO construct?

    – To drive total cost down and increase competition in the private sector

4.2.2 (TLO 1,3,4,5)

TLOs

  • 1. Identify the members and responsibilities of a public shipyard project team and Regional Maintenance Center maintenance team.
  • 2. Identify typical execution phases of a repair availability and what goes on within each phase.
  • 3. Identify the terms associated with scheduling a maintenance availability.
  • 4. Recognize the roles and responsibilities of the Naval Supervising Activity (NSA) and Lead Maintenance Activity (LMA).
  • 5. Identify organizations that can fulfill the roles of NSA and LMA.

LECTURE NOTES

  • Waterfront CHENG (from NSA) certifies availability completion reports.
  • New work
    • Any additional work that is identified or authorized after contract award that is not related to a work item included in the original contract
  • Growth work
    • Additional work that is identified or authorized after contract award that is related to a work item included in the original contract
    • Some growth work is expected as certain tasks are difficult to fully scope within the

    original contract, e.g., inspection and repair of tanks

  • Anticipated repair work
    • Work that is not fully quantifiable prior to the start of avail (e.g. requires work after inspection), however it is work that is expected and based on historical lessons learned, can be budgeted, resourced, and scheduled prior to the start of avail or inspection
  • Availability Work Certification
    • Objective Quality Evidence (OQE) shows completion was technically correct
    • Gives Navy Supervisory Activity (NSA) with maximum reasonable assurance that availability work is complete and technically correct.

SUMMARY

  • Who directs the project planning and management, job planning, and job sequencing and scheduling processes for public shipyard availabilities?

    – Project Engineering & Planning Manger (PEPM)

  • Which public shipyard management team member is responsible for obtaining funding, identifying authorized work, and working directly with customer on issues of cost and schedule?

    – Business & Strategic Planning Office (BSPO)

  • Who is the RMC management team member that serves as the maintenance team

    leader? – Port Engineer

  • What are the typical execution phases of an availability?

    – Arrival, rip‐out & repair, open & inspect, progress assessment, testing, trials & certification

  • Who is responsible for certifying work accomplished during an availability?

    – Naval Supervising Activity (NSA)
