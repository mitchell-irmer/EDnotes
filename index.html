3.1.5 (TLOs 4,6,7,10)

TLOs

  • 1. Recognize the types of cost contained in the different cost categories.
  • 2. Recognize the two types of financial management systems.
  • 3. Identify activities that are typically “Mission Funded” (General Funded (GF)) and those that are typically Working Capital Funded.
  • 4. Recognize how the General Fund financial system operates.
  • 5. Recognize the schedule for creating and reviewing an Expense Operating Budget (EOB) for a General Fund funded activity.
  • 6. Recognize the definition of Navy Working Capital Fund Terminology (Net Operating Result and Accumulated Operating Result).
  • 7. Recognize how the Navy Working Capital Fund (NWCF) System operates.
  • 8. Identify the general criteria an activity must satisfy to become part of the Navy Working Capital Fund.
  • 9. Recognize the schedule for the development of a budget for a Navy Working Capital Fund activity.
  • 10. Recognize the major factors used to determine a stabilized labor rate.
  • 11. Identify the documents used to task and transfer funds and the conditions for their use.

LECTURE NOTES

  • General Fund = Mission Fund
  • General Fund (PPBE funded)
    • Financial Goal: adequate for mission but not excessive
    • "checking account"
  • Navy Working Capital Fund
    • "credit card"
    • funding from customers, not apportionment
    • Financial Goal: set prices so that revenue and expenses break even over time.
  • General Fund Characteristics
    • Appropriated funds are distributed via chain of command to organizations to fulfill their designated purpose (mission)
    • ASN (FM&C) is the Echelon I activity that allocates funds to heads of major commands at Echelon II level
    • Echelon II comptrollers manage funds and are referred to as Budget Submitting Offices (BSOs). BSOs manage and distribute funds to lower echelons in the form of allotments and Expense Operating Budgets (EOBs)
    • Echelon III organizations provide allowances and operating targets (OPTAR) to their subordinate commands
  • Mission activities can also receive funds from other activities in exchange for items or services. (Reimbursable funds)
    • Direct costs only (labor and material, travel, etc)
  • What is the NWCF?
    • A revolving fund that operates like a commercial business but focused on mission requirements instead of making a profit.
  • Criteria for inclusion in NWCF
    • activity has an identified output
    • must have an accounting system to collect cost
    • customers paying are identified so resources are aligned to the right account
    • evaluated buyer-seller relationship pros and cons
  • Stabilized Labor Rates (SLR)
    • Charged to customers to reimburse NWCF
      • required to be established during budget process to recover
        • budgeted costs of good and services and all G&A/overhead
        • Prior period gains/losses
        • approved surcharges
      • Provides budget stability to customers
    • SLRs are set to generate a breakeven Accumulated Operating Result (AOR) of $0 in the Budget Year.
      • Customer rates are increased or decreased based on Net Operating Result (NOR) and to achieve zero AOR in the budget year

SUMMARY

  • A NWCF command is financially successful if

    – Revenue and expenses break even over time (AOR is close to zero) – Pricing is affordable

  • What are the components of the stabilized labor rate?

    – Direct labor

    – Indirect costs

    – AOR recoupment

  • A command is considering sending NAVFAC a funding document to re‐carpet the building using its O&MN funds. It is currently August and it is estimated that the carpeting project will start in September and end in November. What type of funding document is appropriate and why?

    – Project Order

    • Work will not be complete until after the expiration of the O&MN funds

LECTURE NOTES

  • Life‐Cycle Cost (LCC) can be defined as the total cost to the Government of a program over its full life (cradle to grave)
  • Program Life‐Cycle Cost Estimates (LCCE) are important because:
    • They form the basis of program budgets and are also critical in the day‐to‐day management of a program
    • Current acquisition regulations require programs to maintain a program cost estimate
    • They support headquarters‐directed analyses, to defend program budget requirements, or to choose between potential Contractors in source selection
  • Cost Analysis Requirements Description (CARD)
    • Developed by the Program Office to support cost estimates
    • Includes a description of features pertinent to costing the system
    • Quantitative descriptions of technical, physical, programmatic, and performance characteristics
    • Requirements are specified in DoD Manual 5000.04 (series)
    • Updated for all milestone reviews
  • Learning Curve Theory
    • As the quantity produced of a product doubles, the per unit production hours expended in producing the product will decrease at a fixed rate or constant percentage
  • Should Cost management opportunities
    • Learning curve improvements
    • Process improvements
    • Changes in contracting
    • Competition
    • Incentives
    • Gov furnished equipment (GFE)
  • Should Cost – don't include these
    • straight percentage reductions
    • large investments with no near-term cost recovery

SUMMARY

  • (TRUE/FALSE) Life‐Cycle Cost (LCC) is the Acquisition Cost + O&S + disposal cost

    – True

  • Which cost estimating technique would work best:
    • Very early in life‐cycle for an initial estimate and there was a similar program in the past

      • Analogy

    • Working on preliminary design. High‐level technical and performance parameters are known and there are applicable known cost estimating relationships

      • Parametric

    • Detailed design is complete and a detailed WBS is available

      • Engineering

    • Getting ready to start the 2nd production run

      • Actuals

  • (TRUE/FALSE) Assuming a 90% learning curve, material cost for the 8th unit should be 90% of the material cost of the 4th unit

    – FALSE (Learning curve applies to LABOR ONLY)

  • What 2 factors need to be considered to adjust constant year dollars to then‐year dollars?
    • Inflation
    • Outlay rates

    • Total Cost Input (TCI) = Direct Cost + Overhead Costs
    • G&A rate = G&A Costs/TCI
  • Why are forward pricing rate agreements used?

    – To provide reasonable projections to estimate and budget for the costs of contracts

LECTURE NOTES

  • Configuration Management (CM) Definition:
    • Configuration management is a technical and management process applying appropriate resources, processes, and tools to establish and maintain consistency between the product requirements, the product, and associated product configuration information
    • It provides a complete audit trail of decisions and design modifications
    • helps avoid costs and reduce risks
  • Configuration Item (CI) definition:
    • An aggregation of hardware, software, or both, that is designated for configuration management and treated as a single entity in the configuration management process. The entity within a configuration that satisfies an end use function and that can be uniquely identified at a given reference point
    • Designation as a CI increases visibility and management control
      • Cost can't be the reason for designation as CI
  • ILS - Integrated Logistical Support
    • Documentation, spare parts, training, reliability, usability, correctness.
    • cost and schedule impacts for configuration changes
  • The current approved configuration is the approved baseline plus approved changes from that baseline

SUMMARY

    – Avoid cost, reduce risk, control design changes

  • What are the 4 functions of CM?

    – Identification, status accounting, control, verification and audits

  • Designating items as Configuration Items requires more:

    – Oversight and control What are the 3 types of configuration baselines? – Functional, allocated, product

  • What are the 3 types of changes?

    – Engineering changes, waivers, deviations

  • The current approved configuration is the:

    – Approved baseline and all approved changes

  • What are the roles and responsibilities for the Interface Control Working Group (ICWG)?

    – Identifying, documenting, and controlling all functional and physical characteristics of the interfaces – Produces Interface Control Documents

  • Changes to requirements, design, and production processes can have impacts across multiple levels of the system and have major negative implications to:

    – Cost and schedule

  • What are the steps to an effective Design Review?

    – Plan, familiarize, individual review, team review, resolve, follow‐up

  • What are the impacts to configuration management when commercial item are used?

    – Government does not control design details – Detailed technical data package may not be available – Requires careful definition of interfaces – Support strategies must address repairs and updates in commercial environment – Test strategies must ensure performance not only at item level, but in integrated units

    • Forecast and evaluate the potential operational effectiveness, suitability, and cyber survivability of the system during development using experimental models, prototypes, modeling, or simulation

    • Validates system meets operational requirements (prior to M/S C)

    • Large complex programs will often have multiple OAs during EMD phase
    • OAs are typically required to support a Low Rate Initial Production Decision

    • Additional work that is identified or authorized after contract award that is related to a work item included in the original contract
    • Some growth work is expected as certain tasks are difficult to fully scope within the

    original contract, e.g., inspection and repair of tanks

  • Anticipated repair work
    • Work that is not fully quantifiable prior to the start of avail (e.g. requires work after inspection), however it is work that is expected and based on historical lessons learned, can be budgeted, resourced, and scheduled prior to the start of avail or inspection
  • Availability Work Certification
    • Objective Quality Evidence (OQE) shows completion was technically correct
    • Gives Navy Supervisory Activity (NSA) with maximum reasonable assurance that availability work is complete and technically correct.

SUMMARY

  • Who directs the project planning and management, job planning, and job sequencing and scheduling processes for public shipyard availabilities?

    – Project Engineering & Planning Manger (PEPM)

  • Which public shipyard management team member is responsible for obtaining funding, identifying authorized work, and working directly with customer on issues of cost and schedule?

    – Business & Strategic Planning Office (BSPO)

  • Who is the RMC management team member that serves as the maintenance team

    leader? – Port Engineer

  • What are the typical execution phases of an availability?

    – Arrival, rip‐out & repair, open & inspect, progress assessment, testing, trials & certification

  • Who is responsible for certifying work accomplished during an availability?

    – Naval Supervising Activity (NSA)
